================================================================
BEAT 1 -- THE COPPER STORY, HONESTLY (statewide context)
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Start with the news. What's this about landlines going
away?

JEFF:
So AT&T got permission from the federal government to
retire its old copper landlines -- the classic
plug-in-the-wall phone -- for about a hundred eighty
thousand homes across California.

SHAWNA:
A hundred eighty thousand. Should people here be worried?

JEFF:
Here's the honest answer, and it's good news locally:
Nevada County is NOT on that list. I checked AT&T's actual
filing -- our wire centers aren't in it. And even where it
does happen, nothing changes before June of twenty
twenty-seven, and no court has ordered anything. So this
is a slow, statewide shift -- not a switch getting flipped
on your house next week.

SHAWNA:
Okay -- so why give it a whole episode?

JEFF:
Because it points at something that IS true for a lot of
us. That old copper line has one superpower: it powers
itself, from the phone company. So when a storm or a
public-safety power shutoff knocks the electricity out for
days, the copper phone still dials nine-one-one. The
newer replacements -- wireless and internet phones -- need
house power and a strong signal to work.

SHAWNA:
And that's exactly what we lose in an emergency.

JEFF:
Exactly. And I'm not editorializing here -- this is in
AT&T's own paperwork. Their filing admits that their
replacement service won't power things like medical alert
devices, and that with no electricity it will, quote,
"not work, including emergency nine-one-one."

SHAWNA:
That's straight from AT&T.

JEFF:
Word for word. So the takeaway isn't panic -- it's
preparation. Whatever phone you rely on, let's make sure
it actually works where you live and when the lights go
out. That's the rest of the show.

BEAT 2 -- WHICH OF THE 3 NETWORKS WORKS AT YOUR HOUSE
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Let's get practical. If someone's cell service is bad at
home, where do they start?

JEFF:
Start by throwing out the coverage maps. There are really
only three big cell networks -- Verizon, T-Mobile, and
AT&T -- and every one of them will show you a map that
says you're covered. Out here, those maps are computer
guesses. They can't see the ridge behind your house or the
trees over your road. They're modeled -- not measured.

SHAWNA:
So how do you find out the truth?

JEFF:
You measure it. And you can do it for free. Our county
library lends out mobile internet hotspots -- little pocket
boxes -- and they carry them on all three networks.

SHAWNA:
The same hotspots we talked about in the connectivity
episode.

JEFF:
The very same. So here's the trick: borrow one, bring it
home, and see how it does right where you actually live.
Then, if you can, try one on a different network. You can
test all three -- at your kitchen table -- before you ever
pay a cell company a dime.

SHAWNA:
I love that. What if you want the real signal number, not
just the little bars?

JEFF:
The bars lie -- they're marketing. Every phone has a
hidden "field-test" setting that shows your true signal as
a real number. And there's a free app called CellMapper
that shows you which cell tower you're actually reaching,
and which direction it's in. We put the exact how-to on our
website so you don't have to memorize it.

[POST: PROGRAM CARD -- "Test all 3 networks free: borrow a
library hotspot. Nevada County Library -- Hotspots."]

BEAT 3 -- MAKE IT BETTER (the boost ladder)
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Say you test it and the signal's weak but it's there. Can
you make it stronger?

JEFF:
Usually, yes -- and there's a ladder from cheap to
pricier. Cheapest first: a directional antenna. It's like
cupping your hand to your ear, but for cell signal -- you
aim it at the tower and it pulls in a lot more. That feeds
a home internet device -- a hotspot or a router with a
place to plug the antenna in.

SHAWNA:
And the next rung up?

JEFF:
A signal booster -- brand names like weBoost or Cel-Fi.
That grabs the signal outside, amplifies it, and
rebroadcasts it inside so your phone works through the
whole house.

SHAWNA:
Here's the question everyone's thinking: do these actually
work?

JEFF:
They can work really well -- but there's one honest catch,
and it's the whole ballgame: a booster can only amplify a
signal that's already reaching your property. It can't
create one out of nothing. If you get zero signal even
standing outside, a booster won't help -- that's when you
look at a taller antenna mast, or satellite internet like
Starlink. So test first, then buy. Don't guess.

[POST: PROGRAM CARD -- "Boost weak signal: directional antenna
-> hotspot/router -> booster. A booster needs SOME outside
signal -- it can't create one."]


================================================================
BEAT 4 -- THE POWER-USER TIER (60-90s taste; points to website)
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Now -- I know some of our viewers are the handy type who
want to really dial this in.

JEFF:
Then there's a whole deeper level, and I'll give you the
30-second version. That distant tower that actually reaches
you? It's usually broadcasting on a low frequency -- a
"low band" -- because low frequencies travel farther and
push through trees. With the right home device you can
"lock" onto that low band so your gear stops hopping to a
closer-but-useless one. And you match your antenna to that
exact frequency and aim it right at the tower.

SHAWNA:
That's more than a 30-second job.

JEFF:
It is -- so we're not going to walk through all of it on
camera. We wrote the full step-by-step guide -- finding
your tower, reading its band, locking it in, picking the
right antenna -- and put it on our website. If that's your
kind of project, it's all there, free.

[POST: PROGRAM CARD -- "Power-user guide (find your tower +
band, lock it, match the antenna): techempower.org"]


================================================================
BEAT 5 -- KEEP IT WORKING IN AN OUTAGE (resilience)
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Let's come back to the scary part -- the power's out for
three days. My internet phone is dead. What now?

JEFF:
This is the most important beat in the show. Remember why
copper was special -- it powered itself. You can give your
modern setup the same superpower: put your hotspot or
router, and your booster, on a battery backup. A small
one, ideally topped up by a little solar panel or a
portable power station.

SHAWNA:
So the gadget that keeps you connected doesn't die with
the grid.

JEFF:
Right. That's the difference between "my phone works" and
"my phone works when I need it most." And one more thing:
if you're ever moved off a copper line onto a wireless
replacement, ask them for a backup battery, and
plan for longer than a day,
because our shutoffs can run several. Test your setup
during a calm afternoon -- flip the house power off for an
hour and make sure you can still call out.

SHAWNA:
Practice before the emergency.

JEFF:
Every time.


================================================================
BEAT 6 -- AFFORD IT, HONESTLY
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Now the money. Some of this gear isn't cheap. Is there help
paying for it?

JEFF:
I'm going to be straight with you, because that's the whole
point of this show. There used to be a federal program
that knocked thirty dollars off your internet -- the
Affordable Connectivity Program. It ran out of money and
ended. There's no direct replacement right now.

SHAWNA:
And LifeLine -- the discount we've talked about?

JEFF:
LifeLine is still here and still worth getting -- it's a
monthly discount on phone or internet SERVICE. But it pays
for service, not equipment. It won't buy you an antenna or
a booster. So I won't pretend there's a coupon for the
hardware -- there isn't, today.

SHAWNA:
So what CAN people lean on?

JEFF:
The free stuff, and it's real. The library hotspot -- free
to borrow, and it doubles as your test device. Local
nonprofits that hand out refurbished computers and phones.
And here's an idea I'd love a local group to run with: a
lending library for a signal kit -- an antenna, a hotspot,
a battery -- that neighbors could check out the way they
check out that hotspot. Nobody's doing it yet. It's a great
project for a service club or a library.

SHAWNA:
Planting a seed.

JEFF:
Planting a seed.
