SEGMENT ONE: CALFRESH -- GROCERY MONEY YOU'VE ALREADY EARNED
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Let's start with the anchor. Jeff -- CalFresh, one sentence.

JEFF:
CalFresh is California's grocery money program -- what folks
used to call food stamps. You get a card -- they call it an
E-B-T card -- that works like a debit card at the register,
it reloads every month, and you spend it on groceries at the
stores where you already shop.

SHAWNA:
And who qualifies? Because I know the answer is "more people
than you think" -- it's the name of the show.

JEFF:
It really is. In California, most households can earn up to
about TWICE the federal poverty line and still qualify. In
real numbers: about twenty-six hundred dollars a month for
a single person, before taxes. About fifty-three hundred a
month for a family of four.

SHAWNA:
Fifty-three hundred a month. That's a lot of working
families watching right now.

JEFF:
Exactly who it's for. And here's what it's worth: depending
on income, a single person can get up to two hundred
ninety-eight dollars a month, and a family of four up to
nine hundred ninety-four. Most folks land somewhere in
between. And even the minimum -- twenty-four dollars a
month for one- and two-person households -- buys real
groceries every single month.

[VERIFIED July 1 against the USDA FY2026 COLA memo PDF
(saved with the fact-check artifacts); figures good through
Sept 30, 2026 -- routine week-of confirm only]

SHAWNA:
Myth time -- because I've heard this one for years. "I'm on
S-S-I, so I can't get CalFresh."

JEFF:
False -- and it's been false since twenty nineteen. S-S-I,
that's the monthly federal check for seniors and people
with disabilities living on very little. For decades,
California really did lock S-S-I recipients out of food
benefits -- so a lot of folks "know" the answer is no. That
rule is GONE. Since June of twenty nineteen, you can get
both. If somebody told you no years ago, it's a new answer
now.

SHAWNA:
And seniors in general get a friendlier set of rules, right?

JEFF:
Three ways. If your household has someone sixty or older,
the strict up-front income cap doesn't apply -- the county
only looks at what's left after key bills, the net number.
Second, out-of-pocket medical costs over thirty-five
dollars a month actually raise your benefit. And third, a
senior who can't shop or cook on their own because of a
permanent disability can sometimes count as their OWN
household, even while living with family. So don't rule grandma out just
because she lives with you -- ask when you apply.

SHAWNA:
Okay -- the honest part. The rules changed this year,
didn't they?

JEFF:
They did, and we'll always be straight with you. Two
changes from last year's big federal law. First: since June
first, able-bodied adults eighteen through sixty-four need
to work, volunteer, or train about twenty hours a week to
keep benefits long-term. But hear the second half: the
exemption list is long. Parents of kids under fourteen,
anyone pregnant, anyone medically unable to work,
caregivers, students, and more. Don't assume the work rule
hits you -- apply, and the county figures out which rules
apply to you.

SHAWNA:
And the second change?

JEFF:
Immigration status. As of April, some immigrants who are
here lawfully -- including refugees -- lost FEDERAL
CalFresh. California does run a state version, the
California Food Assistance Program, with the same benefit
amounts -- but it only covers certain groups, like
green-card holders in their first five years and survivors
of abuse. Right now it does NOT cover most refugees who
lost the federal benefit. So if anyone in your household
isn't a citizen: don't guess, and don't panic. Using food
benefits won't hurt a green-card application -- but get
free advice from a legal-aid nonprofit first. And the food
bank never asks about status.

[WEEK-OF RECHECK: verified July 1 vs live CDSS H.R.1 FAQ;

SHAWNA:
So how does somebody actually apply?

JEFF:
Four doors -- pick whichever you like. Online, any day of
the year, at BenefitsCal dot com. By phone -- the statewide
number is on your screen. In person at a county office --
Nevada City or Truckee, both on your screen. Or just
dial two-one-one, and a real person walks you through it.
Two more things. If you're in a true emergency -- almost no
money and no food -- say so, because there's an expedited
track that can load benefits within three days. And once
you're on, it's not forever-automatic: answer the county's
renewal mail so your benefits keep flowing.

SEGMENT TWO: WIC -- NOT JUST FOR MOMS
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Program two: W-I-C. And I'll confess something -- I always
assumed W-I-C was only for moms.

JEFF:
Almost everybody does, and that myth costs kids real food.
W-I-C stands for Women, Infants, and Children, and it's
free monthly money for healthy groceries -- loaded on its
own card, like a debit card. It's for anyone pregnant,
anyone who's had a baby in the last six months,
breastfeeding parents, and any child under five. And here's
the part almost nobody knows: dads, grandparents, foster
parents -- if you're raising a child under five, YOU can
enroll that child. Grandma raising a grandkid? That kid can
get W-I-C.

SHAWNA:
So the child qualifies no matter who's doing the raising.
What about income?

JEFF:
Higher than people guess, and it counts everyone in your
household. A household of one, about twenty-nine thousand
five hundred a year. And careful with that number -- a
single parent and one child already count as a household of
TWO, and that limit is about forty thousand. A family of
four, about sixty-one thousand a year.

SHAWNA:
And you said there's a shortcut.

JEFF:
The best shortcut of the day. If you or your child already
have Medi-Cal -- that's California's free or low-cost
health coverage -- or CalFresh, or CalWORKs, another state
aid program, then you have ALREADY passed the W-I-C income
test. Automatically. And bring your Medi-Cal card to the
appointment -- it doubles as your proof of income.

SHAWNA:
And what does a family actually get each month?

JEFF:
Two things on that W-I-C card. First, specific healthy
staples -- milk, eggs, cereal, beans, baby formula, baby
food. Second, flexible produce money for ANY fruits and
vegetables you want -- fresh, frozen, or canned:
twenty-six dollars a month for each kid ages one to five,
forty-eight if you're pregnant, fifty-two if you're
breastfeeding. Plus
real human support -- nutrition advice and breastfeeding
help, included, free. And if you're pregnant, enroll as
soon as you know -- the earlier you start, the more months
of help you get.

SHAWNA:
Twenty-six dollars of produce a month, per kid -- that
adds up fast. Okay: where do folks sign up?

JEFF:
It's run by county Public Health, and it's an appointment,
not a waiting line -- phone, video, or in person, your
choice. There's an office in Grass Valley and one in
Truckee, and both numbers are on your screen. One call, and
they'll tell you exactly what to bring.

SEGMENT THREE: FOOD THIS WEEK -- LITTLE OR NO PAPERWORK
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Last stop. Everything so far is an application. What about
somebody whose fridge is empty TONIGHT?

JEFF:
Then skip the forms -- this county is genuinely good at
this. Two heroes. First: the Interfaith Food Ministry, on
Henderson Street in Grass Valley. It works like a
drive-through: you pull up, tell the volunteers your picks
of the day, and they load your car -- fresh produce, dairy,
pantry staples. If you live anywhere in Nevada County and
you need food, you qualify. Full stop. There is NO income
test. Bring a photo ID and something with your address -- a
bill or a lease -- and you can shop once every other week.
They're open Monday, Wednesday, and Friday from ten to one
-- heads up, the first hour is by reservation, walk-ins
start at eleven -- plus Saturdays, no reservation needed.
And if you're unhoused -- no address -- you are still
welcome. Just say so at check-in.

[POST: PROGRAM CARD -- Interfaith Food Ministry: 440
Henderson St, Grass Valley / 530-273-8132 / Mon-Wed-Fri
10 to 1 (first hour by reservation) + Saturdays]

[VERIFIED July 1 on IFM's own site: Saturdays confirmed

SHAWNA:
And hero number two?

JEFF:
The Food Bank of Nevada County -- and this one asks for NO
paperwork at all. Nothing. No forms, no income questions.
They bring the food to you, with mobile distributions all
over the county -- second and fourth Thursdays in Grass
Valley, every Tuesday in Truckee, plus more sites from
North San Juan out to Washington. You show up, you get
groceries. And if you physically can't get to a site, they
partner with a local group called FREED to deliver to
homebound folks at home.

SHAWNA:
And parents -- there's a summer thing happening right now,
isn't there?

JEFF:
Every summer, the food bank runs a free summer lunch
program -- any child age two to eighteen, on weekdays, at
sites all around the county. No registration, no paperwork,
no income anything. Kids just show up and eat. This year it
runs through the end of July, and the current sites are
always at foodbankofnc dot org.

SHAWNA:
One more group before we close: seniors.

JEFF:
Two quick ones for folks sixty and up. If cooking or
shopping has gotten hard, Meals on Wheels delivers real
meals to your door -- Gold Country Senior Services covers
the west side of the county. In Truckee, Sierra Senior
Services delivers to seniors who mostly can't get out of
the house -- and if that's not quite you, they'll set you
up with a group meal instead. Either way, one phone call

suggested. Nobody is turned away who can't pay. And if you'd rather eat with
company: the Sierra Gold Senior Center on Colfax Avenue in
Grass Valley serves a hot lunch at noon every Monday,
Tuesday, and Thursday. Sixty and up, just show up -- on
your first visit they'll have you fill out one quick form.
