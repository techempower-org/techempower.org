================================================================
MYTH ONE: "BENEFITS ARE ONLY FOR THE TRULY DESTITUTE"
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Let's jump right in. Jeff -- aren't these kinds of benefits
just for folks who are truly destitute? People with nothing,
people experiencing homelessness?

JEFF:
No. And honestly, Shawna, that's the single biggest myth out
there -- so let's kill it with one real number.

People hear "low income" and assume it means almost nothing.
Here's the truth: the official poverty line for a family of
four this year is thirty-three thousand dollars. And many
assistance programs consider you low income at up to DOUBLE
that -- sixty-six thousand dollars a year for a family of
four.

SHAWNA:
Sixty-six thousand. That's... a lot more than people picture
when they hear "low income."

JEFF:
And for housing help, it goes even higher. The federal
housing agency -- it's called HUD -- draws its own line,
county by county. Here in Nevada County, their official
low-income line is just over ONE HUNDRED THOUSAND dollars a
year for a family of four.

SHAWNA:
Wait -- a household making a hundred thousand dollars, here
in Nevada County, can count as low income for housing
programs?

JEFF:
That's right. And some programs go higher still -- there's
even help for first-time homebuyers. But we'll get to
specific programs all season long. For today, just hold onto
this: every program draws its own line, and almost every
line is higher than people guess.

SHAWNA:
So the answer to "do I make too much?" is...

JEFF:
Don't guess -- check. That's actually the point of this
whole show.

SHAWNA:
But why are those lines so much higher than people expect?

JEFF:
Because the math of everyday life changed. In recent years,
paychecks have mostly kept up with everyday prices -- but the
essentials have not played fair. Housing, healthcare,
childcare -- the things you can't skip -- have grown far
faster than wages.

And the long-run picture is worse. Since the late seventies,
the American economy became about 87 percent more productive.
The typical worker's pay grew only about 33 percent.

SHAWNA:
So the country got richer -- but the paycheck didn't.

[POST: CHART GRAPHIC ON SCREEN -- hosts gesture toward it and hold the beat]

JEFF:
And housing is the worst of it. Look at this chart. A typical
home recently hit a record -- selling for more than FIVE
TIMES what a typical household earns in a year. Twenty-five
years ago, three times was normal.

SHAWNA:
So even folks working full time can find the math just
doesn't work anymore.

JEFF:
Exactly. And that is exactly who these programs are for. Not
"other people." You.


================================================================
MYTH TWO: "I'D BE TAKING IT AWAY FROM SOMEONE WHO NEEDS IT MORE"
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Okay, myth number two -- and I hear this one from the kindest
people. "If I sign up, aren't I taking benefits away from
someone else? Someone who needs it more than me?"

JEFF:
This is the myth I most want to bust, because it stops
generous, conscientious people from getting help they've
earned. The answer is no -- and here's why.

Let's name the big programs, because we'll be talking about
them all season. CalFresh -- that's California's grocery
money, what folks used to call food stamps. Medi-Cal --
that's free or low-cost health coverage. The Earned Income
Tax Credit -- extra cash back at tax time for people who
work. And S-S-I -- monthly checks for seniors and people
with disabilities living on very little.

Here's the thing about all four: there is no fixed pot of
money. The law says if you qualify, the help is yours by
right -- and the funding GROWS to cover every eligible
person who signs up. Your benefits do not come out of your
neighbor's pocket. That's literally how the law is written.

SHAWNA:
So there's no line I'm cutting in.

JEFF:
For the big programs, no line at all. And here's the part
that gets me. Every year, an estimated SIXTY BILLION dollars
or more in benefits goes unclaimed across this country --
because eligible people never apply.

SHAWNA:
Sixty BILLION dollars?

JEFF:
Every year. Right here in California, about one in five
people who qualify for CalFresh grocery help aren't getting
it. And about one in five workers who've earned that
tax-time cash back never claim it.

SHAWNA:
That's people's own money, just... left on the table.

JEFF:
Left on the table. Now -- one honest exception, because
we'll always be straight with you on this show. A few programs DO
have limited funding and waiting lists. The big example is
Section 8 -- the federal program that helps pay your monthly
rent. But even there, applying doesn't take help away from
anyone who already has it. It just gets you in line. And if
you never apply, you're not in line at all.

One more thing. If a program accepts you as eligible, that
means you fit the exact group that program was built to
serve. These eligibility rules are strict. If you pass them,
you're not cheating the system -- you ARE who the help is
for.


================================================================
MYTH THREE: "GOOD PEOPLE DON'T TAKE HANDOUTS"
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Here's the myth people don't say out loud, but everybody
feels. "Why would I deserve free or reduced-price things?
Isn't accepting handouts... bad?"

JEFF:
Let me reframe that. The help you'd get from these programs
is a tiny drop in the bucket compared to the help and money
that wealthy organizations and people receive from the
government every single year. If you think quote-unquote
"good people" or wealthy people don't take government
support -- you're flat-out wrong. Let me give you three
examples.

Example one: big corporations. Researchers tracked hundreds
of big corporations that stayed profitable every single year
for five straight years. On average they paid about 14
percent in federal income tax -- not the 21 percent the law
says. About one in three of them paid ZERO in at least one
of those years. And a few did even better than zero.

SHAWNA:
Better than zero? What does that even mean?

JEFF:
Twenty-three of them, over all five years combined, paid
nothing at all -- zero, or even less. Some literally got
money back. All of it perfectly legal: tax breaks for buying
equipment, for how they pay their executives, for doing
research. And Congress made those breaks bigger and
permanent just last year.

SHAWNA:
So a billion-dollar company can legally pay zero, but a
working family feels guilty about food assistance.

JEFF:
That's the absurdity, yes. Example two: bailouts. When big
industries hit a crisis, the government steps in -- every
time. In 2008, Congress put up seven hundred billion dollars
in emergency rescue money for the banks -- and out of that
same pot, the carmakers GM and Chrysler got tens of billions
too.

SHAWNA:
And that wasn't a one-time thing.

JEFF:
Not even close. When the pandemic grounded air travel,
passenger airlines got about fifty-four billion to keep
paying their workers. And in 2023, when a big bank called
Silicon Valley Bank collapsed, the government stepped in so
every person and business with money in that bank got every
dollar back -- even amounts way above what the government
normally protects. Nobody called those CEOs freeloaders.

Example three -- and this one's about your coworkers. Back
in 2020, a government watchdog agency studied who was
actually receiving food assistance and Medicaid -- Medicaid
is the national name for what we call Medi-Cal here in
California. In every state they looked at, the top employers
of people on those benefits were household names. Walmart
was number one. McDonald's usually right behind. And about
seven in ten of those folks were working FULL TIME. Wages
have come up since then -- Walmart says its average store
worker now makes about eighteen twenty-five an hour.

SHAWNA:
And here in Nevada County, covering the basics for just one
adult takes over twenty-seven dollars an hour.

JEFF:
Exactly. So working full time at a big-name employer and
still qualifying for help isn't a personal failure -- it's
the system working exactly as designed.

SHAWNA:
So when someone skips benefits they're eligible for, out of
pride...

JEFF:
They're giving up a tiny bit of money on principle, while
the wealthiest one percent -- who hold roughly a third of
all the wealth in this country -- take everything they can,
with zero shame. If you want the full story, there's a
brand-new book about exactly this: "Escape from Capitalism,"
by the economist Clara Mattei. Her point is simple: when
benefits for working people get cut, that's a political
choice, not a law of nature. So take what you've earned. The
wealthy already do.

[POST: BOOK COVER ON SCREEN while Jeff mentions it --

================================================================
MYTH FOUR: "IT'S SCARY -- WHAT IF I DO IT WRONG?"
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Last myth, and it's the quiet one. A lot of people tell us:
"It's scary to hand all my information to a government
agency. And what if I apply, mess something up, and get
punished for it?"

JEFF:
Yeah. That fear is real, and I want to honor it -- and then
take it apart.

First, the good news. Most benefit programs have strict
privacy and security rules, written into federal law. Your
information is only supposed to be used to run the program
itself.

Now, the honest part -- because we promised to be straight
with you. The current federal administration has rolled some
of that back, and people should know about it. In 2025, ICE
-- the immigration enforcement agency -- was given access to
Medicaid's records: the list of everyone signed up. That's
about seventy-nine million people nationwide. California and
twenty-one other states sued. Right now, for Californians, a
court order says only basic information can be shared -- no
medical records. And only about people who aren't here
lawfully. That fight is still in court.

SHAWNA:
So what does that mean practically, for someone watching?

JEFF:
For most people, applying is still safe -- full stop. But if
anyone in your household isn't a citizen, do one thing
first: get free advice from a legal-aid or immigration
nonprofit. And know this. Immigration officials use
something called the "public charge" rule when they decide
green cards. Under that rule, as it stands today, using food
help, health coverage, or housing assistance does NOT count
against you.

SHAWNA:
Okay -- and the "what if I do it wrong" fear?

JEFF:
Here's the truth. If you apply honestly and it turns out you
don't qualify -- nothing bad happens. Nothing. The worst
outcome is a "no." There's no penalty for being told no.

Just two things to keep in mind. One: always answer
truthfully. Being WRONG is fine -- honest mistakes are not
fraud. Knowingly giving false information is the one thing
that does carry penalties. Two: if a program ever overpays
you by mistake -- even THEIR mistake -- they can ask for that
money back later. So when your income changes, report it
promptly, and you'll never be surprised.

SHAWNA:
And if someone applies and then changes their mind?

JEFF:
You can take back your application at any time before
there's a decision -- no penalty -- and apply again later
whenever you want. Already enrolled? You can ask to close
your case any time. You are in control of this process the
whole way through.

Look -- it's scary to do anything new. But this is the kind
of scary that might actually end with money in your pocket
and food on your table.
