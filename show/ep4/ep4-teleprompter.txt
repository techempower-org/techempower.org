SEGMENT ONE: THE ENERGY BILLS
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Start with the bill everybody dreads. Jeff -- the power
bill.

JEFF:
Then let's start with the one you sign up for ONCE. It's
called CARE -- California Alternate Rates for Energy -- a
permanent discount on your P-G-and-E bill. Twenty percent
or more off -- and on the electric side it often runs
around a third off. Every month, as long as you qualify.

SHAWNA:
And who qualifies?

JEFF:
Two doors, and you only need one. The income door: a
household of one or two can make up to about forty-three
thousand a year -- a family of four, sixty-six thousand.
Or the automatic door: if anyone in your home is on
Medi-Cal -- the health coverage -- or CalFresh, the grocery
money, or W-I-C, or S-S-I, you already qualify. No income
math. Enrolling takes minutes, online or by phone, no
documents up front, and the discount just shows up on the
bill.

SHAWNA:
And if someone makes a little too much for CARE?

JEFF:
There's a second program right above it called FERA --
Family Electric Rate Assistance. Eighteen percent off the
electric side -- about forty-five dollars a month for a
typical household. A family of four can make up to about
eighty-two thousand and qualify. And a myth to bust: FERA
used to be closed to households of one or two people. That
rule is GONE -- singles and couples now qualify. One
application covers both programs, and P-G-and-E puts you
on whichever one fits.

SHAWNA:
Okay. And for someone who's already behind on the bill --
not struggling with next month's, but BEHIND?

JEFF:
Two answers. If you're on CARE or FERA and you're ninety
days or more behind, ask P-G-and-E about their debt
forgiveness plan: pay your current bill on time for twelve
months, and each month they erase a twelfth of the old
debt -- up to eight thousand dollars, gone.

SHAWNA:
And answer two?

JEFF:
The heating-bill bailout: LIHEAP -- the Low Income Home
Energy Assistance Program. A once-a-year payment on your
energy bill, up to fifteen hundred dollars, plus separate
crisis help if you're holding a shut-off notice. And
listen, foothills folks: it covers PROPANE, WOOD, and OIL
-- not just the power company. It never has to be paid
back, and renters qualify too, even if utilities are
folded into your rent. Locally it's run by a group called
Project GO -- the number's on your screen, press two for
Nevada County. One honest note: the money is first come,
first served each year, so call and confirm funds are
still available. And their voicemail only takes about
forty calls a day -- so call first thing in the morning.

SHAWNA:
Now -- you said free upgrades. To the house itself.

JEFF:
Energy Savings Assistance. P-G-and-E will come to your

weatherstripping, L-E-D lighting. If your fridge is
fifteen years old or more, they may replace it. And if you

Same income rules as the CARE and FERA discounts, renters
welcome with the landlord's okay, and the home just needs
to be at least five years old. And there's a SECOND free
weatherization door -- the state's Weatherization
Assistance Program, run by that same local agency, Project
GO. Don't worry about which is which: one call to Project
GO, and they sort out which program covers what.

SHAWNA:
It's August in fire country -- I have to ask about power

JEFF:
Three quick ones for shutoff season. If anyone in your
home relies on a medical device that needs electricity,
get on P-G-and-E's Medical Baseline program -- that one is
about medical need, NOT income: more power at the lowest
rate, and extra warnings before a fire-weather shutoff.
Some Medical Baseline folks qualify for a FREE backup
battery -- P-G-and-E reaches out if you're pre-qualified,
but you don't have to wait: call two-one-one, or FREED
here in Grass Valley, and ask about the backup battery
programs. Supplies are limited, so ask early.
And if you're on CARE or FERA, there's up to five hundred
dollars back on a generator or battery. We live in fire
country -- but you don't have to face it alone.

SEGMENT TWO: HOUSING -- THE HONEST VERSION
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Okay. Housing. And Jeff, we promised people honesty on
this show, so -- Section Eight. What's the real story in
twenty twenty-six?

JEFF:
Here it is. Section Eight -- officially the Housing Choice
Voucher -- is the big federal rent program: you pay about
thirty percent of your income, and the voucher pays the
rest, straight to your landlord. For our county it's run
by the Regional Housing Authority. And the honest part:
their Section Eight waiting list is CLOSED right now. Not
"long" -- closed. Today, you can't even get in line.

SHAWNA:
So what do you tell somebody who was counting on it?

JEFF:
Three things. Watch the housing authority's website --
lists reopen with little notice, and you want to apply the
DAY it opens. The site's on your screen. Second: don't
make a voucher your only plan -- even open lists can take
years. And third: separate from Section Eight, some
individual apartment buildings keep their own low-income
waiting lists -- they're on that same website, each with
its own contact. Call the property manager and ask what's
open.

[POST: PROGRAM CARD -- Regional Housing Authority:
regionalha.org / 888-671-0220]

[WEEK-OF RECHECK: Section 8 waitlist still closed? -- can

SHAWNA:
And for somebody in crisis -- not "waiting list" trouble,
but "where do I sleep TONIGHT" trouble?

JEFF:
Then don't research -- call two-one-one. That's the front
door to the county's whole housing-crisis system: one
call, they screen you, and every partner agency can pick
you up from there. If you need shelter tonight in western
Nevada County, it's Hospitality House in Grass Valley --
call the shelter line FIRST, it's on your screen, and
they'll walk you through check-in, which runs every day
from four to five-thirty in the afternoon. On the Truckee
side there's now a year-round Navigation Center with
overnight beds -- it opened this February -- and
two-one-one can connect you the same day.
Families on CalWORKs -- the state aid program for families
-- can get up to sixteen nights in a motel, once a year,
through BenefitsCal, the state benefits website from our
food episode. And there's more -- targeted help for
veterans, for families, for folks fleeing violence -- and
two-one-one knows all of it.

SEGMENT THREE: THE CAR
================================================================

SHAWNA:
The car. Up here it's not a luxury -- it's the job, the
groceries, the doctor. Where's the help?

JEFF:
Four pieces, quick. Piece one: the smog check. If your car
is a ninety-six or newer and it FAILS smog, the state's
Bureau of Automotive Repair can put up to fourteen hundred
fifty dollars into the repairs. Income-qualified -- about
thirty-six thousand a year for one person, about
seventy-four thousand for a family of four. Straight talk: you pay a
co-pay of at least twenty percent of the repair bill, and
the work happens at a state-certified smog shop -- they're
called STAR stations.

SHAWNA:
And if the car's just... done?

JEFF:
Piece two: the state will BUY it. Vehicle retirement:
if the car just FAILED its smog check, that's thirteen
hundred fifty dollars, no income test at all. And if
you're income-qualified, it's up to two thousand -- even
for a car that passed. Both of these run on a state budget
year that just restarted in July, so the money's as fresh
as it gets.
Apply at the state site on your screen.

[POST: PROGRAM CARD -- Smog repair + vehicle retirement:
bar.ca.gov/cap]

[WEEK-OF RECHECK: BAR CAP new-fiscal-year funds still
available; income caps refresh with the poverty guidelines
-- re-pull current figures]

SHAWNA:
Now the new one you found -- insurance. Because liability
insurance is the law, and it is NOT cheap anymore.

JEFF:
Piece three, and almost nobody's heard of it: California
Low Cost Auto insurance -- a state-run program. A YEAR of
liability coverage for roughly two hundred to under a
thousand dollars, depending on your county -- and rural
counties like ours sit near the LOW end. You need a valid
California license, a car worth twenty-five thousand or
less, and income under about thirty-nine thousand for one
person -- about eighty-two thousand for a family of four.
Even brand-new drivers can qualify. Now the honest part:
it's liability ONLY. It covers the other person's car and
injuries -- not your own car -- and the limits are lower
than a standard policy. But it keeps you legal, insured,
and on the road -- for hundreds instead of thousands.

[POST: PROGRAM CARD -- CA Low Cost Auto Insurance:
mylowcostauto.com / 1-866-602-8861]

SHAWNA:
And piece four?

JEFF:
Piece four comes with a Market Match-sized asterisk.
There's a state program called Driving Clean that can put
serious money -- up to ten thousand dollars -- toward
swapping an old polluting car for a cleaner used one. But
parts of it are closed right now, and the funding comes
and goes -- so check the website, or ask us on Discord,
before you count on it. While we're being honest: that
two-thousand-dollar state e-bike voucher you may have
heard about? It ended last December -- don't wait on it.
And one more, for western-county P-G-and-E customers --
Truckee, you're on your own utility -- if you're
income-qualified and buy a used electric car, P-G-and-E
has a four-thousand-dollar rebate.

SEGMENT FOUR: GETTING AROUND WITHOUT A CAR
================================================================

SHAWNA:
And if there is no car -- or it's in the shop -- what's
real, here, today?

JEFF:
More than people think, and it got cheaper this year. The
bus system has a new name -- Nevada County Connects, the
one folks knew as Gold Country Stage. A local ride is a
dollar fifty, three dollars on the longer regional routes,
transfers free. And the headline: since last November,
anyone seventeen or UNDER rides completely free. No
paperwork -- kids and teens just get on the bus.

SHAWNA:
And seniors?

JEFF:
Sixty-five and up ride half fare with a discount card from
the transit office -- bring photo ID, and note the gotcha:
a MediCARE card works as proof, but Medi-CAL is a
different card. Same half fare if you have a disability.
But here's my favorite fare in the county: if you're
eighty or better, the Golden Ticket -- FREE on all the
regular routes, for LIFE. Proof of age, that's it.

SHAWNA:
Free for life at eighty. I love that. And for folks in
between -- too young for the deals, income's tight?

JEFF:
There's a Low-Income Pass -- six months at a time,
renewable -- that takes the bus fare off your shoulders,
through a referral from FREED, the local
independent-living nonprofit, or county Social Services,
or Behavioral Health. Ask any of them -- or two-one-one.

SHAWNA:
What about somebody who can't use a regular bus at all?

JEFF:
Door-to-door service: Nevada County Now -- it used to be
called Gold Country Lift. If a disability keeps you off
the regular bus, you apply and get certified, and you book
those rides the day before -- no same-day -- with the
return booked at the same time. Seniors sixty-five and up
get something more flexible: an on-demand dial-a-ride. One
simple application -- you can even hand it to the driver
on your first trip -- and you can call for a same-day ride
if there's room, though the certified rides get first
dibs. Either way, it's four dollars each way.

Truckee side?

JEFF:
Truckee, you win this round: TART -- the Tahoe Truckee bus

county-wide: if you're on Medi-Cal, rides to medical and
dental appointments are a free benefit -- one phone
number, book at least five days ahead. They'll even
reimburse gas money for a friend who drives you.
