# You Qualify for More Than You Think: TechEmpower Launches a Free Two-Minute Benefits Check, a Fact-Checked Video Series, and Two Free Apps

## A family of four can earn $66,000 a year — for housing programs, over $100,000 — and still qualify for help in Nevada County. The Grass Valley nonprofit's rule for all of it: no number goes public until it has been verified against the primary source.

**GRASS VALLEY, Calif. — [DATE]** — TechEmpower, a Grass Valley 501(c)(3),
today announced three free tools to help Nevada County households claim
benefits they already qualify for: an anonymous two-minute check at
**techempower.org/qualify**; **"Wait, I Qualify?!"**, a myth-busting video
series produced with Nevada County Media; and two free, open-source apps,
**Candela** and **Forage for All**, now on Google Play.

An estimated $60 billion or more in benefits goes unclaimed nationally every
year, largely because eligible people never apply. One reason: "low income"
reaches higher than most assume. Many assistance programs use twice the
federal poverty guidelines — $66,000 a year for a family of four in 2026 —
and HUD's 2026 low-income limit for housing programs in Nevada County is
$100,100 for a family of four.

"People hear 'low income' and count themselves out," said Jeff Hein,
TechEmpower's founder. "What we see isn't somebody gaming the system — it's a
family skipping grocery money, health coverage, and utility discounts they
were eligible for all along, because nobody told them. Check before you
decide you make too much."

The two-minute screener asks seven questions, in English and Spanish, and
checks a household against 33 verified programs — CalFresh, Medi-Cal, WIC,
PG&E bill discounts, free phone service, and more. It runs entirely on the
user's device; answers are never transmitted or stored. Every dollar figure
shows its source and verification date, and automated checks block any
number not re-verified within the last 120 days.

The show is held to the same standard. Every claim in every script is checked
against primary sources — the Federal Register, HUD, and USDA among them —
then attacked by a second, adversarial review before taping. Episode 1,
"You Qualify for More Than You Think," takes on four myths that stop people
from applying: [VIDEO-URL]. New episodes land on the first and third Mondays.

Two apps round out the launch. **Candela** reads TechEmpower's guides — or
any document, e-book, or web article — aloud in natural neural voices
generated on the device, with no account, no ads, and no tracking; it doubles
as the teleprompter for the series' companion shorts. **Forage for All** is a
community map of fruit trees and edible plants on public land, with
approximate locations by default and no trackers. Both are free on Google
Play ([PLAY-LINK-CANDELA] · [PLAY-LINK-FORAGE]); Forage for All also runs in
the browser at forage.techempower.org.

"None of it asks who you are — no sign-up, no tracking; your answers stay on
your phone," Hein said. "And if we can't prove a number from the primary
source, it doesn't ship. People deserve help and their privacy — not one
traded for the other."

Households can also dial 2-1-1 — in Nevada County, 211 Connecting Point — or
visit findhelp.org: free, confidential, English and Spanish.
