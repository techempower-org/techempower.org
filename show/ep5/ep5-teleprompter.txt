SEGMENT ONE: MONEY TO START A SMALL FARM
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Start with the farm dream. Someone's watching right now
with an acre out back and a plan they keep scribbling on
napkins. Where does the money come from?

JEFF:
From the U-S Department of Agriculture -- and this
surprises people. Their Farm Service Agency -- the
U-S-D-A's lending arm -- makes what they call a
microloan: up to fifty thousand dollars to start or run a
small farm, with an application that's been stripped down
on purpose for first-timers, small operations, and the
niche stuff -- market gardens, organic growers, even
beekeepers. And there's no minimum, either: if all you
need is a few thousand dollars for fencing, that's a
perfectly good microloan.

SHAWNA:
Okay -- but what does a federal loan want from someone
who's never farmed?

JEFF:
Less than you'd fear -- but more than a napkin. Here's
the honest version. You do NOT need to own land: a lease
works fine. You do NOT need years of farming behind you:
an apprenticeship, or working with an approved mentor,
can count in place of experience. What you DO need is
real. A written business plan. A credit history you can
defend -- there's no minimum score, they look at whether
you pay people back. And collateral -- which can be the
crop, the livestock, or the equipment the loan itself
buys. Interest runs around five percent right now -- it
resets every month, so check the current rate -- and you
can take up to seven years to pay it back.

SHAWNA:
What will it actually pay for?

JEFF:
Almost everything a small operation needs: seed, feed,
livestock, bees, fencing, hoop houses, irrigation, tools
-- even the cost of getting certified organic.

SHAWNA:
And if the dream is bigger -- actually BUYING a farm one
day?

JEFF:
There's a sister version of the microloan for exactly
that: same fifty-thousand-dollar cap, pointed at buying
or improving a farm instead of running one. The
experience bar is higher on that one, so most folks start
with the operating loan and grow into it. And one honesty
note that surprises people: these loans exist for farmers
who CAN'T get a regular bank loan yet. That's not a
loophole -- that's the program working as designed.

SHAWNA:
And once somebody HAS land -- improving it?

JEFF:
That's federal door number two: a cost-share program
called E-QUIP -- the Environmental Quality Incentives
Program -- run by the U-S-D-A's soil-and-water agency.
You agree on a conservation project -- irrigation
upgrades, livestock fencing, a high tunnel to stretch the
growing season, clearing brush for fire safety -- and the
government pays you back up to seventy-five percent of
the cost. If you're a beginning farmer -- and your first
ten years running a farm count as beginning -- that can
rise to as much as ninety percent. And beginners can get at least half the
money UP FRONT, before the work starts, so you're not
floating it. This isn't theory, either: that program has
put more than four million dollars into Nevada County
land since the nineteen-nineties.

SHAWNA:
So where's OUR door for all this federal money?

JEFF:
Closer than you'd think. For the conservation money, the
soil-and-water folks keep a real office in Grass Valley,
on Presley Way -- a district conservationist who answers
the phone. The number's on your screen. For the loan,
offices are regional -- so use the office locator at
farmers dot gov, or dial two-one-one and say the word
"farm."

SEGMENT TWO: THE LOCAL FARM DOOR (+ OPTIONAL GUEST)
================================================================

SHAWNA:
So that's the federal money. But between a napkin plan
and a loan application there's a lot of learning -- and a
lot of paperwork. Who actually helps with that, here?

JEFF:
Two local doors, and I love them both. Door one: Sierra
Harvest -- the Nevada County food nonprofit -- runs a Farm
Institute whose whole job is launching farmers. Mentors
who actually farm here. Grant coaching -- real humans who
help you chase farm money. And that's not a brochure
promise: the federal government gave them about
three-quarters of a million dollars to do exactly this
for beginning farmers in our region.

SHAWNA:
And you told me something earlier about LAND. Because
that's the part I can't picture -- land is the expensive
part.

JEFF:
This might be my favorite program of the whole season.
It's called Land Match. There are landowners in this
county sitting on good, cleared, watered ground they
don't use -- and Sierra Harvest plays matchmaker. You
tell them what you're looking for, and they connect you
with a neighbor's idle land. The deal itself is between
the two of you -- sometimes a regular lease, sometimes
lease-to-own, sometimes a work trade where the rent gets
paid in vegetables. Nobody is handing out free land. But
since twenty sixteen, by their own count, they've matched
twenty-nine farmers onto more than a hundred acres -- and
thirteen farm businesses have launched out of that work.

SHAWNA:
People in this county are farming ground they don't own.

JEFF:
Right now, this season. And if you're the one SITTING on
idle ground -- same number. They'd love to hear from you.
Then there's the classroom piece: their Farm Biz course
-- six weeks, online, budgeting and business planning.
That's where people build the exact business plan the
microloan application asks for. It runs each winter, so
the move TODAY is to get on their interest list. Classes
are low-cost, and there's a scholarship application if
the fee is a problem.
Door two: the University of California's farm advisors --
Foothill Farming, the cooperative extension serving
Placer and Nevada counties. Workshops and field days,
free or cheap, taught by the university's own advisors --
and once a year, a two-day Beginning Farming Academy, a
crash course in starting on a sound footing. Their Grass
Valley office is at the Veterans Memorial Hall on South
Auburn Street -- open Tuesdays and Thursdays -- and the
number's on your screen.
And don't just take our word for it: the county's own
farmer-resources page points new farmers to these same
two doors.

SHAWNA:
One more -- say the garden's already overflowing. What if
somebody just wants to SELL what they grow?

JEFF:
Then your door is the county's own agriculture
department, up on Maidu Avenue in Nevada City. That's the
office that handles the farmers-market and farm-stand
certificates, organic registration, even beekeeper
registration. When the hobby is ready to become a
business, they'll tell you exactly what you need -- that
number's on your screen too.

SEGMENT THREE: THE NONPROFIT SAFETY NET
================================================================

SHAWNA:
Okay -- from the land to the safety net. You promised me
local nonprofits doing things nobody believes are
actually free.

JEFF:
Two of them. Both real, both one phone call. First:
FREED, the independent-living nonprofit in Grass Valley
-- you've heard us mention them before on this show.
Here's the service that stops people cold: equipment
reuse. Donated medical equipment, checked over and given
away FREE. Wheelchairs. Walkers. Shower chairs. Hospital
beds. And more. If you have a disability -- any age -- or
you're an older adult, you call, you tell them what you
need, and if they have it, it's yours. No bill. And if
they don't have your device on the shelf, they can point
you to California's statewide reuse listings to track one
down. Think about what a hospital bed costs a family that
just brought somebody home from the hospital. The
number's on your screen.

SHAWNA:
And the free lawyer. Because when I hear "free lawyer," I
assume there's a catch.

JEFF:
The only catch is what kind of case. Legal Services of
Northern California -- their Auburn office covers Nevada
County -- is free civil legal aid. That's the stuff that
wrecks families: an eviction notice. A landlord who won't
fix the heat. A benefits denial -- CalFresh, Medi-Cal,
S-S-I. A debt collector who won't quit. Clearing an old
record that's blocking a job. It's free if money's tight
-- and if you're sixty or older, just call. There's a
special senior program, and the intake folks will tell
you exactly what you qualify for. What they DON'T do:
criminal defense, divorce and custody, personal injury.
So call for the civil stuff -- and there's even an
evening intake line if you work days.

SHAWNA:
Season finale, Jeff. Land the plane -- and start with the
secret you've been sitting on all year.

JEFF:
All season, every single episode, we've said the same
three digits: dial two-one-one. Today you finally meet
who's behind them. It's called Connecting Point -- and
it's not some faraway national call center. It's a public
agency your own counties created, headquartered right
here in Grass Valley. Since two thousand three it's been
connecting folks to in-home care, and in twenty eleven it
took on the county's two-one-one line. And the phone line
is just one program: this same agency matches in-home
caregivers with folks who need care at home, runs job
help for parents on CalWORKs, and puts on free caregiver
trainings and wellness classes. All of it local. Free,
confidential -- and they know every program we've covered
this season, plus the ones we never got to.

SHAWNA:
Five episodes. Give us the season in one breath.

JEFF:
Episode one, we busted the myths: this help isn't
charity, it's yours -- and more than sixty billion
dollars of it goes unclaimed in this country every single
year, not because people don't qualify, but because they
never find out they do. Episode two, we got you online.
Episode three, food on the table. Episode four, we kept
the lights on and the wheels turning. And today: the
neighbors who catch you, and the land that might just
feed you. Some of that sixty billion has your name on it.
It still does.

SHAWNA:
So if somebody watched all five episodes and still hasn't
made a single call...

JEFF:
Then make it the easy one. Dial two-one-one -- free,
confidential, around the clock, English and Spanish.
Online, go to findhelp dot org and type in your ZIP code.
And us: techempower dot org -- every episode of this
season lives at techempower dot org slash show -- and
click "Join our Discord," our free online chat community,
where a real neighbor helps you one-on-one. Whatever door
you pick -- pick one. One call, one ZIP code, one click.
That's the whole homework -- and it's the last homework
we'll ever assign you.

SHAWNA:
Five episodes, and no tease this time -- just this. To

JEFF:
Thank you for letting us into your living rooms all

might be surprised.
